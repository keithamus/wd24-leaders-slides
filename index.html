Web Architectures

Today

& Tomorrow

This is a narative of the last few decades from one UI engineers view, one set of opinions. Not those of my employer.

Software Crisis

In 1968, NATO hosted a software development conference to address what was deemed a Software Crisis at the time. Increasing complexity of hardware meant that software projects were often overbudget, over time, inneficient to build, maintain and run. Sound familiar? They gathered prominent software developers of the time, such as Edsgar Dijkstra and Doug Mcillroy to present on ways to solve these issues.

MASS PRODUCED SOFTWARE COMPONENTS

BY M.D. MCILROY

Source http://homepages.cs.ncl.ac.uk/brian.randell/NATO/N1968/index.html
Mcillroy presented a talk on Mass produced software components, proposing that, much like hardware, the software industry should be able to produce reusable parts to build software cheaper, faster, and easier.

Or as mcilroy put it software engineers could "consult a catalogue offering routines in varying degrees of precision, robust- ness, time-space performance, and generality".

How does this apply today?

I don't think Mcilroy was talking about components in the same sense we do today, I think he was talking about libraries, and decomposable systems. Composable primitives. Mcilroy worked at Bell Labs on the unix operating system, which embodied these ideas.

But why am I talking about a conf talk that happened over 50 years ago?

Well these are the same ideas that exist throughout the industry, including UI and the Web. Mcillroy's talk focused around the growing complexity of hardware, moving from vaccum tubes to mainframes, but we're experiencing the same increase in complexity of hardware as more software development pivots from desktop to mobile, new user interface paradigms, new input devices like touch, vision.

The problem is we're putting so much energy into building these experiences over and over, I think this is down to a failure of the web platform.

The Web Platform

In terms of a decomposable system, the Web Platform has historically struggled. It gives us the bedrock for which to place our libraries and components atop, but it hasn't always composed very well, and so we essentially ship our own web platforms on top of the web platform.

The Web at its inception in 1990, it was just for documents. The system wasn't designed for apps, just pages.

And so the history goes; the 2000s however, there was a big push to create richer documents and applications using the Web. The W3C was excited about this new XML format they'd invented but meanwhile browser companies like Opera, Apple, Mozilla and even Google wanted to focus on innovating and evolving the web as it stood. HTML5, CSS2, Google Chrome. Engines got faster and started a benchmark race with sunspider, kraken, octane. Loosely this falls under the web 2.0 umbrella.

  1. 1990s: Documents

  2. 2000s: Applications

  3. 2010s: ...?

  4. 2020s: ...?

So one way to look at these decades is 1990s was the rise of the document, while 2000s was the rise of the app. 2001 saw the release of the iphone which enabled these new experiences on the web change happened at a rapid pace.
This problem was kind of unique to the 2010s, which became decade of innovation through experimentation of JavaScript frameworks and libraries. Each of this new libraries brought their own paradigms of how to build apps and organise code. They filled the gaps missing in the platform.

But they've also veered toward a convergance. While they all originally offered something quite different in their inception, many have converged around a handful of major patterns.

The rise of JavaScript and this struggle for a decomposable web platform gave rise to these frameworks but the costs have not been properly realised; their are externalised costs which have yet to be realised. Browsers have become vessels for this JS virtual machine and little else, and so little of the platform is actually used, it seems.

This cost, the cost of compute and the cost of network get pushed onto our users; it's not just about data deserts or fringe users who disable JS. I don't need to tell Australians about the latency of servers hosted in us-east-1; the speed of light is bound by the laws of physics.

But also theres a misinterpretation of moore's law, we're not getting better phones which lessen the cost of this. Alex Russel's work speaks more to this but our devices aren't getting faster, there's just more of them. If you have an iphone in your pocket you're part of the elite, your device is many times faster than the median, and the median is flatlining.

Scaling to your next zero, your next order of magnitude of users, gets more difficult as you dive into this long tail and the costs are more acutely evident. If this is alien to you, you might not be measuring the right things.

Your technical teams might be talking about SSR vs client side rendering, especially due to the costs of good sold on the server, but this is yet more user experience arbitrage, to avoid the real problem we need to talk about which is the cost of this JS.

  1. 1990s: Documents

  2. 2000s: Applications

  3. 2010s: Frameworks

  4. 2020s: ...?

So going back to our mapping of decades, maybe the 2010s could be documented as the era of the frameworks that scaffolded an architecture around our codebases, exploring how best to fill the gaps of the platform.

What is the result of this? Are we going to keep this frankly obnoxious complexity around forever? I think the convergance lets us see a path forward where leveraging the platform can help us lower our dependency on such high cost patterns and get more for free.
2013 was somewhat an epochial moment for the web, perhaps our 1960s software crisis. In 2013 members of the W3 cosigned to tighten the feedback loop between platform development and the industry, by allowing newer low level primitives to serve as building blocks as new experiences and decompose large systems into components that helped explain how the pieces got put together.

And so while innovation in userland progressed with frameworks browsers have been getting more capable to address these needs.

Extensible Web Manifesto

Service Workers

HID abstractions (Pointer Events)

Device APIs (WebSockets, WebUSB, WebBluetooth)

Exposed browser APIs (MutationObserver, ResizeObserver, IntersectionObserver)

Raw exploratory APIs, CSSOM, CSS Houdini, Paint API

Web Components (Custom Elements, Templates)

So the fruits of the exensible web manifesto lead to many new innovations in the browser:

Service workers to control the network
New input apis like pointer events
Low level APIs like web usb
Additionally exposing features that browsers have had internally such as the observer APIs
New exploratory APIs like CSS Houdini and the paint APIs.
The list goes on. One big addition around this time was the Web Components APIs, allowing developers to extend HTML elements into their own custom widgets. This arguably solved the View part of the Model-View paradigm.

Object.observe


            function listen(records) {
              console.log('object changed', records)
            }
            const o = Object.observe({}, listen)
            o.foo = 1
But views are nothing without their models, and one of the more disappointing failures of this era was Object.observe. This was designed around the idea of tracking object mutations. You'd pass an object. The model, for the view. It was ultimately deemed too complex and not the right fit when it came to framework use, and so frameworks live on, unable to be dethroned...

Reactive Observables

Meanwhile frontend frameworks were converging around one pattern of reactive Observables for managing state. Frameworks like Angular, and libraries, rxjs, mobx, have all converged on this idea of Reactive programming.

The idea of bringing this into the platform has been kicked around since 2015, at various standard groups but I think this is starting to become a reality. 

            let o = new Observable(subscriber => {
              subscriber.next(1)
            })
            o.subscribe({
              next: v => console.log(v)
            })
          

            element
              .on('click')
              .filter(e => e.target.matches('.foo'))
              .map(e => ({ x: e.clientX, y: e.clientY }))
              .subscribe({ next: handleClickAtPoint })
And so this is now a proposal to land this feature into the platform.
But if we look at the top frameworks by popularity, a lot of them have also converged on other find grained reactivity primitives, otherwise known as Signals. Aside from React, all the major frameworks have converged around Signals for state management.

Svelte calls them Runes, Ember calls them computed properties, but they're the same thing. Unless you're React, signals is the Model part of the view problem. Even React has integrations of signal-like libraries: Recoil, Jotai. 

          const count = ko.observable(0);
          const doubleCount = ko.pureComputed(() =>
            count() * 2
          );
It's not a new idea. But these ideas can often take a while to percolate. KnockoutJS originally popularised this idea in 2010, with the idea of an observable value and a computable side effect. 

          const count = new Signal.State(0);
          const doubleCount = new Signal.Computed(() =>
            count() * 2
          );
But now there's also an effort to standardise these at TC39, to specify this built-in to the language. Members of popular frameworks like Angular, Ember, Preact, QWIK, Solid, Svelte, Vue, and more are all collaborating on building out a universal protocol for Signals that will be baked into the language.

Closing the value add gap of frameworks

So I think a large gap that frameworks filled is now being pushed down into the platform.

Global client side state managent which was the central pitch of the frameworks in their inception is starting to look like a solved problem. 

            <template parts>
              <p>Hello {{name}}<>
            </template>
There's nascent discussions and proposals around integrating data mutations right into HTML too, with "DOM Parts" which could end up taking Signals as a feed-in mechanism. There's a lot to solve here so I'm not going to go into too much detail but you can dive into the proposal on the QR code.

This is more a vision of the future; where data binding becomes an intrinsic part of HTML. All of the puzzle pieces are starting to come together here, and so I'm hopeful we'll see the gap being closed here.
Going back to the 2000s though, we also saw the industry maturing in design. "Design Patterns" and "Design Systems" that evolved from branding guidelines and brand identity. UI libraries like YUI and jQuery UI popularised this idea of off-the-shelf component libraries that have now extended into the design systems where we see branding and interactivity combined, into a new vernacular with which applications speak to their users.
And we can't talk design without talking about CSS, which has also made huge strides at catching up to the innovation of the decade; not just the language through features like variables and nesting, but also whole new technological advancements like View Transitions that allow for declaring transitions between pages to create seamless experiences, blurring the line between MPA and SPA, if there ever was a line in the first place.
But design systems aren't just about styling pages or branding them to a companies colour pallete. It is about creating that language of interactivity. Something like Google's Material design system is arguably the most used because it coincides with the design language of the android operating system. Systems like Material design have invented new paradigms of interactivity like the floating action button.

But again these come at high operational cost. Big companies are pouring millions of dollars into these libraries and these teams continuously firefight for their positions, as rigour goes up the team finds it harder to deliver at the pace of application engineering, because they're solving for the whole problem space, reinventing the wheel over and over.
But isn't just giant companies throwing their weight around. Web Awesome, a component system by the Font Awesome team, just crowdsourced nearly three quarters of a million USD to continue building their open source design system which used to be called Shoelace. There is a clear and evident need for making this kind of development easier.
Source https://www.inwebson.com/html5/html5-form-with-css3-markup/
But looking at something like built-in forms and form styling, it's not hard to see why a lot of these design systems exist.
In 2019 when Microsoft switched Edge from using the EdgeHTML & Chakra to using Chromium, Greg Witworth lead the forms team to restyling and modernising some of the available form controls. The improvements were soreley needed, but also subtle. They solved key accessibility issues and gave us more tolerable defaults. More bold actions needed to be taken though.
At the same time as improving the basics, Greg founded the Open-UI Community Group. An initiative to look at how we can close the gap on components like we have done other technologies.

By far and away the largest source of frustration when developing form controls has been the select element. So we asked ourselves: why are people rebuilding select? What are they trying to get from it? How can we decompose select to its constituent parts so that there is less re-work across the industry.
And so there's been a huge amount of research gathering information on all the components all of these design systems are building, and cataloguing it, figuring out the missing pieces, and attempting to add them back in.
The result is a journey of breaking down anatomy of a select and creating new capabilities so that it's easier to customise the built-in select but also easier to create your own variants. We've been slowly extending what's possible with select with small incremental changes, such as allowing hr in selects. 

            <select style="appearance:base-select">
              <button>
                <selectedoption></selectedoption>
              <button>
              <datalist>
                <option><img src="./one.png"></option>
                <option><img src="./two.png></option>
              </datalist>
            </select>
And so we're working on a way where you can inject different pieces of HTML to replace bits of the select element. Want a custom button? Stick a button tag inside a select. Want to customise the selected option text? The new selectedoption element will act as that placeholder. Datalist will drive the custom container, we're extending options to have images, systemtically addressing all of the pain points raised by design systems and developers. And select is just the first, in this journey.
This should not require pulling in 30kb of JavaScript to make happen. At worst this should be a few hundred lines of CSS and some additional markup. 

            <button popovertarget="my-popover">
              Open Popover
            <button>
            <div id="my-popover" popover>
              <p>I'm a popover</p>
            </div>
But it's not just about extending what's there, it's also about providing the base primitives to allow for rolling your own versions easier. Popover allows you to create floating UI which sits above all content on the page on something called the top-layer, and is immune to overflow, positioning, or z-index issues that is common when creating this kind of UI. It eliminates the need for globs of JavaScript, even the opener buttons are declarative, meaning you get accessibility for free too!

If you've ever used something like React Portals; you can stop, popover solves this problem. RIP React Portals we hardly knew ye.

And this isn't just contrived code, this works today - this markup is accessible! No aria-details needed, no focus management, it does it all for you!
This is usable today. In fact you've probably used it without knowing it; we've been migrating all of the floating UI at GitHub to using popovers and dialogs and it's literally making 1000s of lines of code redundant.
While OpenUI is looking at these low level pieces, we're also exploring the high level. Some of you may have read Brad Frost's piece on a Global Design system, one that captures some of this need to provide bigger building blocks and a more cohesive system atop HTML. OpenUI has commited to exploring this further, we want to evolve this space and work towards tighter integration loops to feed work back into browsers.

OpenUI is an open community! So come join in! We welcome your participation.

So let's send it home, all of these new web platform advancements happening in the 2020s:

We're on the road to state management with observables and signals.

A real shot at templates and data binding landing in the web.

Custom Elements; a built in component paradigm growing more complete by the day, more on that in my Web Directions Code talk.

CSS is now as expressive as SASS, and more powerful, with things like view transitions.

More extensible form controls, customisable select!

Popovers and dialogs solving modern UI challenges.

And investing into high level problems to enable teams to move faster.

Alex Russel has a seminal article, The Market for Lemons, in which he lambasts the 2010s as "the lost decade" for the web. I think the article gets a lot right, and it's well worth a read but I'm slightly more optimistic about where the decade has lead us.

  3. 2010s: Frameworks

  4. 2020s: Realignment

  5. 2030s: Renaissance

So for me, I look at the work that has been done and will be done, and I see a bit of a renaissance on the web platform. I think while 2010s were a period of discovery and a huge growth period for frameworks, I see the coming decades as a return to form.

I see the 2020s as something of a re-alignment to the platform. I think we'll see a decline of framework moats, meanwhile the web platform will encompass more of the value add that current frameworks provide.

And I think as we close out the decade and all of these technologies are enshrined into the platform and widely available, into the 2030s we might see a jQuery moment for frameworks; they'll likely continue to innovate but I think there will be less compelling reason to bring in all the client side JS and the Web Platform will be seen more as the framework that it is. Perhaps in response we'll see more of a shift to the server for these things, and that's maybe another talk brewing...

Always bet on the web

Ok so how can you act on this? What are the real next steps? Set your teams up for success by measuring the cost of your platform, finding the hidden externalised costs, costs of goods sold and the scalability challenges. Having a serious conversation about this can help your team scale to the next zero.

But there's readily available pieces for you to poise yourself ready for the next decades, tech like popovers and view transitions reduce the gap between the value a frameworks provide. Signals and Observables can be prospectively adopted with polyfills... but you can use your voice and lobby for this work to continue! Advocate for this change. If you're an IC or TPM, join us at open-ui.org. This work cannot continue without tight feedback loops with industry partners.

Let's continue having this conversation of where to take the web. It's our platform, we're all stakeholders and we can expect more from it. Change might be slow but careers can be long.
